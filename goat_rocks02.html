The next day I planned to climb Old Snowy Mountain and traverse the main Ridge as far as I could. I started out I think around 7AM, again with a very light pack with some essentials and lunch.

Even with all my hatred for scree I took this alternate route up to the area I was in yesterday.

Really cool spot here where a glacier retreated.

...and someone left their ramen bowl. This was not the first trash I found and packed out.

It got extremely windy for a short period here, to the point where I put on my hardshell.

My first look at the ridge beyond Old Snowy. Looks doable. (spoiler alert, it was).

I ate a candy bar and got started with the ridge traverse. I heard this was Class 2 and 3 most of the way. I did not know how far I was going to go, but I had a light goal of summiting Ives Peak. I think in my videos I was calling this peak St Ives??? Not sure what happened there. I also really wanted to descend from the ridge and meet back up with the Lily Basin trail instead of backtracking.

There was a light bootpath most of the way, with some more advanced route finding to avoid sketchy sections. The scrambling was a blast. I did not encounter anyone else on this ridge.

Ives was awesome. Perfect stopping point for the ridge run.

Looking back on the entire ridge to the starting point at Old Snowy. Goat Lake also pictured:

I think if I hadn't gone so hard the day before I would have continued. I felt good but knew I needed to pace myself.

I hung out at Ives for nearly an hour, ate lunch and chilled in my Crazy Creek chair (bless this thing).

Then headed down to the saddle where I made that video commenting on the insane Class 5 route. From there I headed west straight down the ridge toward the Lily Basin trail that was easily visible in the distance.

Looking back up at the ridge I just descended.

I suddenly found myself in Snowgrass Flats. Check.

Don't tell Mount Rainier but I think Mount Adams is prettier. No shade whatsoever on Rainier, he just starts to look like a wrinkly old man this time of year, while Mount Adams... PSSSHH *gestures broadly*

Now for some annoying logistics. I had to backtrack quite a bit to get to my camp. This would have been less annoying if I decided to end the day, but it was only 2pm and I figured I could hike out the next day, turning a 4 day trip into a 3 day. If I wanted to do this however, I would have had to do around 18 miles and then drive 3 hours home. I figured I could do some of those miles today since I still had time. So not only did I backtrack to my camp, I tore it down and rebacktracked back up to Snowgrass Flats to continue the loop. I would maybe recommend not doing this and actually doing some light planning, but whatever.

The plus side of this is I got to eat day 3's dinner for lunch. I think it was Pad Thai and it was pretty good.

Leaving the core area behind met back up with the PCT in Snowgrass Flats with a full pack. I was aiming to do at least 5 miles and camp somewhere around Nannie Peak. It was getting into the 80s at this point which made the whole backtracking process more of a slog.

This section was stunning. The trail wrapped around a huge basin beneath the Goat Rocks ridge section beyond where I scrambled to. It looked like the Dolomites.

From the basin was the last climb of the day up to Cipsus pass. This image looks down from it.

More Dolomiti.

I was starting to feel very tired at this point. Jelly legs.

I ended up camping at Sheep Lake. Lots of PCT hikers here, and also lots of campsites. I took one a bit further from the lake.

Ate a second MRE for dinner (first time eating two of those in one day), and after a healthy mixture of whiskey and valarian root to end the day.

The last day was an easy 10 miles descending Nannie Ridge and moving through the forests near Walrupt Lake. This was a major recreation area with car campsites and a big parking lot.

From here, I had to ascend 1500' up to Berry Patch which was not fun. Trail was overgrown, steep, crossed roads, and the grand finale was walking about a mile down a dirt road in 85 degree heat, inhaling the dust kicked up by incoming Subarus. This was a Saturday so the trailhead was packed, with maybe a half mile long line of cars beyond the parking lot.

I think it might be more fun to knock this section out downhill on the first day by doing the loop in the opposite direction.

This region definitely exceeded my expectations. I will try to come back here next year to see the flowers in full bloom. I also saw online you can approach the ridge from the northeast so that might be a good way to hit the sections of the ridge that I missed and see the other side of it. This might be my favorite backpacking trip yet.
