Enchantments Traverse

October 2025 | 1 day | 22 miles | +6000' | 

Caltopo Link

I figured it was time to see if the Enchantments could live up to the hype. I have successfully avoided spoilers of this region for several years, and through various interactions with people who described their experiences there, it has ascended to a sort of mythical status in my mind. I decided some time ago that I would not even venture to Colchuck lake until I was physically able to do the Traverse, ideally in the fall. I was copiously rewarded for my patience.

Since this trail is not a loop, I had planned (unaware of the shuttle service) to stash my bike at the terminus and ride it 8 miles back to the starting point. This is not what I did for reasons I will explain.

I packed very light for this trip, expected temps in the high 30s to low 50s. Everything to the right of the trekking poles in the pic below went with me. In retrospect I would have carried a full set of thermals in case of emergency at this time of year.

The Approach

I started by headlamp from the Colchuck Lake trailhead at 4:30 am.

And arrived at Colchuck by around 7am. First time seeing Dragontail and Colchuck peaks, jaw on the floor, one of the most striking mountains I have ever seen. I hung out here for around a half hour waiting for a bit more light before tackling Asgaard.

Once I got a little more daylight I started moving around the west side of Colchuck Lake in persuit of the infamous Asgaard Pass.

Asgaard Pass

Asgaard Pass, the rite of passage to the Enchantments. 2200' from lake to top.

I've heard and read many accounts of Asgaard. I think it is easy to both over and under-estimate it. While it was very long, steep, and unrelenting, it was not particularly technical or dangerous. I imagine being off route might change that, but I found the ascent to be not quite as bad as I thought it was going to be, while also being extremely hard. Not sure what you can do with that information.

The routefinding was a bit challenging, but the general advice is to stay to the left of the stream and the trees. I encountered light snow and ice in the last 20% of the climb made it a bit sketchier. I slipped twice but was not injured.

The Core

I try not to be hyperbolic, but for all the natural places I have been, this truly felt like stepping off of a ship and onto an alien world, like the game No Man's Sky. The landscape here is incredibly distict.

This was my first time seeing Larches.

Prusik Peak from a distance.

I'm not sure the English language is adequate to describe this area, and these images hardly do it justice, but this is essentially a high alpine basin with many small lakes that mostly consist of snowmelt. The upper basin you first encounter is treeless and very rugged. You then gradually descend into the lower basin where the vegitation thickens, passing lake after lake, each one somehow more picturesque than the one before. On all sides you are flanked by jagged peaks and ridglines most of which appear to have mild approaches. I was so jealous of the many backpackers I passed who had the obscene privilage of leisure, who no doubt were spending their days peak bagging, swimming, and exploring every corner of this region.

I have since learned the success rate of backcountry permits for this region is about 6%. I am starting to understand the hype.

Looking back at the upper basin after descending a bit.

Taking a very short side trip over to Crystal Lake that ended up being one of my favorite spots.

Now heading down further into the basin.

Here the landscape becomes a mix of rock, meadow, and shallow pools, dotted with clusters of larches. Unbelievable.

Prusik Peak. I am curious about it's name considering what that is.

Here I began to see a great deal of wildfire smoke wafting up from the lower basin :(

Heading back

Here is where I decided to turn back and give up on the idea doing the whole traverse. Disappointing but I knew at this point the bike ride was not realistic and the smoke was not very inviting.

Moving back now to the upper basin. I was to gain 1000' and then return like 6200' by the end of the day. I was not looking forward to descending Asgaard but it did sound better than an 8 mile bike ride up 2000'.

I took a slightly different route back to see some new scenery.

This was wild; two lakes barely 20ft apart at different elevations.

I am now again at the top of Asgaard.

Saying goodbye to this alpine paradise. Til we meet again, and we surely will.

The Descent

Getting my ass kicked by Asgaard twice in one day.

But at least I got to see Colchuck in the daylight.

Momma deer and baby deer on the descent from Colchuck.

I got back to the car around 530pm, clocking around 22 miles, and 6000' of gain and loss. I picked up my bike from snow lake, got a burrito from a taco truck in Leavenworth, and headed home.

What a way to end the season. I will 110% be back here next year, permit or not. I have do conclude that the Enchantments do in fact live up to their reputation, and far exceed it.
