Timberline Trail / Copper Spur

September 2025 | 3 days | 39 miles | +11,827' | 

Caltopo Link

The Timberline Trail, the poor (or impatient) man's Wonderland Trail. 3 days circumnavigating Mount Hood with a climb up to 9000' on the Copper Spur.

For some reason I approached this trail thinking it would be a chill and easy hike, with aboslutely no supporting data. If you more than quickly glance at the elevation profile of the route, you'll see that it's entirely up and down, crossing each ridge that extends out from the mountain, one after the other. There were literally zero flat sections of this 40 mile hike. I have paid for my arrogance.

I am being slightly dramatic, it was definitely within my capabilites, I was just not mentally prepared for it.

First time trying bagel and lox on the trail. Turns out smoked salmon keeps for a while.

Day 2

The next day I aimed to climb Copper Spur and make it to the north side of the mountain. I had camped fairly close to the highest point on the loop at 7300'.

But from there my route wanted me to give back 800' to catch the junction to Copper Spur. I just started on a bearing toward it instead of giving back the elevation.

Then 3 stratavolcanoes showed up.

Gaining the ridge, this is where I dropped my pack an continued. I am now on the actual copper spur trail.

Eliot Glacier.

A bit higher than the mapped location of the spur, there was a bivy spot with a register, and a nice monument.

At this point I am still just shy of 9000' feet, so I continue a bit further up. The environment starts to feel more alpine, as the ground starts to turn muddy from glacial run off, and falling rock becomes a hazard.

The highest point I reached at 9100'. The highest I have climbed under my own power.

Day 3

I had already decided the day before that I was going to hike out on this day, and my decision was validated by heavy wildfire smoke that rolled in during the night. I had 18.5 miles to do this day, which would have been fine except that I overlooked the 4500' of elevation gain on the way out. Again, I need to stop skimming zoomed out elevation profiles.

I started the day by hiking down 3000' feet, knowing that each step would be exchanged with another one in the other direction later.

Time was not a concern but I knew this was going to be hard especially since I did 4000' yesterday. W/e challenge accepted.

I did not take many pictures this day beacuse I was focused and it was mostly forests.

I ate lunch at this waterfall I could not remember the name of. Dreading the 4000' ft ascent I was about to tackle after already hiking 12 miles, with no real reward at the end :((((.

I did get some nice views of the west side of the mountain on the ascent. A man told me the lodge had good food so I decided to use that as my motivation.

I was struggling at this point. Blisters and foot pain mainly.

Last pic before arriving at the lodge. In the end I ended up doing 20 miles (missed a turn at one point), 4500' up and 4300' down, which is probably the most intense day I've ever done. I was exhausted but the foot pain was insane, likely due to new insoles. I think you're supposed to break those in slower than I did.

I was passed by many trail runners doing the entire loop in a day. I guess I can suck it up.

The lodge was very busy, I wondered how accomodating they were to people who smelled like me, but luckily I had a change of clothes in the car. I ate at the resturant on the top floor, had something called a BLAT, which was just a BLT with avocado, and some chili and a beer. Forgot to take a pic. The food was mediocre but the service was good and I sat near a window with a full view of the mountain.

This route was interesting but I felt it was more work than was merited. I am definitely interested in the idea of trail running it once I am able, but the most likely reason I will be back here will be to climb Mount Hood. Til we meet again.
